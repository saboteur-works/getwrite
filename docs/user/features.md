# GetWrite Features

GetWrite is a writing workspace built around a simple idea: keep everything for a piece of writing in one place, and stay out of your way while you work. Your projects live as plain folders on your own disk — there's no account, no cloud lock-in, and nothing between you and your words.

This page is a tour of what GetWrite can do and how each feature helps your writing.

## Organize your work

### Projects

A project is a self-contained writing workspace — a single folder that holds everything for one body of work, from a novel to a series of articles. Because each project is just a folder on disk, your writing stays portable, backup-friendly, and entirely yours.

### Project types

When you start a project you can pick a **project type** — a ready-made template that sets up sensible folders so you can start writing instead of arranging structure. GetWrite ships with types for novels, serialized fiction, articles, game writing, and poetry/lyrics, plus a blank option for your own workflow. You can also create and edit your own types.

→ [Project Types](project-types.md)

### Resource tree

Everything in a project — documents, images, audio, and folders — appears in a familiar **resource tree** you can browse, create, rename, and reorganize. Drag and drop to reorder; the order you choose is the order your work appears everywhere else, so the structure you see always matches the structure in your head.

→ [Resource Tree](resource-tree.md)

### Start page

Opening GetWrite shows all your projects at a glance, along with totals for how much you've created. From here you can jump into a project, or create, rename, delete, and compile projects without digging through menus.

→ [Start Page](start-page.md)

## Write

### Rich text editor

When you open a text document, GetWrite gives you a focused rich-text editor designed to get out of the way. You get inline formatting (bold, italic, underline, and more) from the toolbar or keyboard shortcuts, and a live word, character, and paragraph count in the footer so you always know where you stand.

### Headings and editor appearance

Headings are styled per project, so the look of your manuscript matches the work. You can customize the size, font, weight, spacing, and color of each heading level — useful for giving chapters, scenes, or sections a consistent, personal feel. The writing surface itself is tuned for long-form comfort, with generous line spacing that never drops below a readable minimum.

→ [Headings](editor/headings.md) · [Editor Appearance](editor/settings.md)

### Search

Find any document by name and jump straight to it with `Cmd/Ctrl+K` — no scrolling through the tree when you know what you're looking for.

→ [Search](search.md)

## Keep track of your story

### Metadata

Every document can carry **metadata** — a synopsis, notes, workflow status, point-of-view character, story dates, and any custom fields you like (characters, locations, props, and the like). Edited from a collapsible sidebar, metadata lets you capture everything you know about a scene without cluttering the prose, and it powers higher-level views of your project.

→ [Metadata](metadata.md)

### Tags

Project-scoped **tags** let you label documents however you think about them — "key scene," "needs research," "POV: Elena" — and find or group them later. Assign tags from the metadata sidebar.

→ [Metadata → Tags](metadata.md#tags)

### Metadata providers

Folders can act as **metadata providers** (Characters, Locations, Items, and the like), letting you link documents to the people, places, and things in your work. Over time this builds a connected web of your story's entities — handy for keeping a sprawling cast or setting straight.

→ [Metadata → Metadata providers](metadata.md#metadata-providers)

### Metadata queries & smart folders

All that metadata becomes searchable: build a filter from simple chips ("POV is Mara *and* status is Draft *and* word count under 500") and GetWrite gathers every matching document. You can filter on your own fields and on built-in attributes like word count, dates, tags, and which documents link to which. Save a query as a **smart folder** and it becomes a living collection that always reflects what currently matches — perfect for tracking drafts in progress, catching documents missing key metadata, or pulling together every scene a character appears in.

→ [Metadata Queries & Smart Folders](metadata-queries.md)

## See the bigger picture

Writing is only part of the process. GetWrite includes several views that help you step back and see your work from above.

### Organizer view

The Organizer lays out a folder's contents as cards — text previews, image thumbnails, and inline audio players — in the same order as your resource tree. Filter by metadata to focus on just the cards you care about, or flip cards to show their notes. It's a natural way to plan and rearrange at the scene or chapter level.

→ [Organizer View](views/organizer.md)

### Data view

The Data view gives you a high-level read on a project: how many documents it holds, its total word count, and what you've edited recently — a quick pulse-check on progress.

→ [Data View](views/data.md)

### Diff view

The Diff view compares two versions of a document and shows exactly what was added, removed, or changed. When you want to know how a passage evolved, you can see it precisely instead of guessing.

→ [Diff View](views/diff.md)

### Timeline view

The Timeline places dated scenes along a story-time axis, so you can see the order things actually happen in your world — independent of the order you wrote them. Zoom from minutes to decades, color-code by point of view, and spot unintended time jumps at a glance. Set a scene's story date in its metadata and it appears here automatically.

→ [Timeline View](views/timeline.md)

## Protect and finish your work

### Revisions

Every document keeps a history of **revisions** — saved snapshots you can look back at and restore. The newest is the active version; you can mark milestones to protect them permanently, and GetWrite quietly trims old history so it never piles up. Your drafts are never lost to a stray edit.

→ [Revisions](revisions.md)

### Resource types

A resource can be a text document, an image, or an audio file — each handled appropriately. Images show resolution and EXIF details, audio gets a play/scrub widget with duration, and folders group it all together. This lets reference material live right alongside the writing it supports.

→ [Resources & Resource Types](resources.md)

### Compiling projects

When a draft is ready to share, you can **compile** a project — from the start screen or from within the project — to pull your work together into a single output.

→ [Compiling Projects](compiling.md)

### Configuring a project

Each project can be tuned to your workflow: customize the status options your documents move through, choose how much revision history to keep, and set per-project preferences like color mode.

→ [Project Configuration](project-configuration.md)
